01The company

A products business that runs on knowledge as much as tonnage

Pacific Fertiliser is an Australian owned company supplying fertilisers, agricultural minerals and liquid nutrients to the agricultural, industrial, civil and mining sectors. From bulk granular fertiliser out of the major ports to tailored NPK and mineral blends, the business spans supply, custom blending, port logistics and spreading services across eastern Australia, with processing plants servicing a large portion of the eastern states.

That breadth is the point, and the challenge. Every quote, blend, delivery and account touches product specifications, pricing history, freight arrangements, stock positions and customer records. The knowledge exists, spread across the company's internal systems and the experience of long-serving staff. The question the business brought to us was simple: how do we get that knowledge to whoever needs it, the moment they need it?

02The problem

Skilled people spending their day as the search engine

In a business like this, the most experienced people become the lookup layer. A customer calls about a blend they ordered two seasons ago. Dispatch wants to know what went out on a particular run. Someone in accounts is reconstructing the history behind an account query. Each answer exists in the internal database, but retrieving it means the right person stopping what they are doing, opening the right system, and knowing where to look.

None of these interruptions is large on its own. Together they set the ceiling on how much a small, capable team can get through in a day. The goal was never to replace anyone's judgement. It was to stop paying senior people to do retrieval, and let them spend that time on the decisions only they can make.

03The approach

Consultancy first: understand the business before touching the systems

The engagement did not start with software. It started with time inside the business, sitting with the people who answer the phones, run the plants and manage the accounts, and mapping how work actually moves through the company: which systems hold what, who asks whom for what, and where the day's time genuinely goes.

That mapping extended to the current software estate. Rather than proposing a migration or a replacement, we documented the systems Pacific Fertiliser already runs, how its internal database is structured, and where the reliable sources of truth sit. The design principle that came out of that work is worth stating plainly: the technology adapts to the business, not the other way around. The team keeps the systems they know. The agents are a new way in, not a new system to learn.

It also told us where agents would not help. Some questions genuinely belong with a person, and the deployment was scoped to leave those alone. A tool that knows its limits earns more trust than one that pretends not to have any.

04What was built

Chat agents that already know where everything is

The deployment gives Pacific Fertiliser's team Nemoclaw chat agents running locally, connected directly to the company's internal database. Staff ask questions in plain language, in the middle of whatever they are doing, and get answers drawn from the company's own records rather than from the open internet.

Plain-language access to company data

Product, order, customer and operational questions answered conversationally, without needing to know which system or screen holds the record.

Answers grounded in the source

Responses draw on the internal database and point back to the underlying records, so anything worth acting on can be verified in seconds.

Scoped to the role

Each agent sees what its users are entitled to see. Access follows the permissions the business already maintains, not a new parallel set.

Built into the working day

The agents sit alongside existing tools rather than replacing them, so nothing about how the team quotes, dispatches or reconciles had to change.

05Under the hood

Local by design: the data never leaves the business

The defining architectural decision is that everything runs on Pacific Fertiliser's own infrastructure. The agents, the database connection and the conversation history all stay on premises. Customer records, pricing and commercial history are the company's competitive knowledge, and the deployment was designed so that none of it is sent to an external service to be useful.

Database access is read-focused and permissioned, connected through the structures the business already maintains rather than a copy that drifts out of date. Because the agents were integrated with the existing software setup rather than around it, the systems the team relies on remain the systems of record. The agents make those systems easier to reach; they do not compete with them.
