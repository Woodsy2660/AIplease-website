01The club

A one-boat club where every two-hour slot has to be shared fairly

Gold Coast Waterski Club is a member-run club of around sixty skiers sharing a single tow boat across fixed two-hour slots from 7am to 7pm. Every booking on the calendar is the boat: there is no second vessel to absorb a clash, and every person on board has to be named for insurance, whether they ski or not.

Fairness is the club's operating principle, and it is written into a genuinely intricate rulebook. Members book up to seven days ahead, can only rebook the same slot six days out, and unlock a second slot in the same week just 48 hours before it. Guests pay tiered day fees that step up after five visits a season, on top of the flat per-set fuel fee everyone pays. The rules work — when everyone follows them.

02The problem

The system took the money, but the rules lived in people's heads

The club's previous app handled the basics: a calendar and a way to pay set fees. Everything else ran on trust and memory. Nothing stopped a member from booking outside the club's own rules, so enforcement fell to the committee after the fact. Unpaid sets drifted for weeks with no lockout and no reminder. Family bookings scattered across individual accounts, so payments were missed simply because a set never appeared on the payer's own dashboard.

The boat itself had the same visibility problem. Engine hours, fuel levels and refuels were recorded inconsistently, which made it hard to spot when the numbers between one booking and the next didn't add up. The committee wasn't asking for a flashier app. It was asking for a system that knew the club's rules as well as its longest-standing members do, and applied them evenly to everyone.

03The approach

Start from the rulebook, design around the ski day

The build began with the club's own documents: the written booking rules, the fee schedule, and a structured Q&A with the committee to pin down every edge case the paperwork left open — what happens with jump-set bookings, how guest visit counts reset each season, who is allowed to drive. Each answer became an explicit constraint in the data model rather than a note in a manual, so the calendar itself refuses a booking the rules would refuse.

The second design anchor was the ski day itself. A booking is not just a calendar entry: it opens on the water with a starting report of engine hours and fuel, and it closes with an end report, the sets skied, and photo proof of the boat's screen. Modelling that full lifecycle meant payments, fuel tracking and incident detection all hang off the same record, instead of living in separate spreadsheets.

One principle carried through every screen: the rules do the enforcing, not the committee. When the system applies the six-day rule or blocks a booking over an unpaid balance, it does so for everyone identically, and no volunteer has to have an awkward conversation.

04What was built

A booking platform that runs the whole ski day, not just the calendar

The result is a single web app that carries a booking from the moment a member picks a slot through to the boat being reported, refuelled and paid for. Anyone can open the calendar and see availability; only members can book, which lets guests plan a visit without the committee fielding calls.

Rule-aware bookings

The calendar enforces the club's booking windows, same-slot restrictions, 48-hour bonus rule and slot capacity automatically, and names every person on the boat for insurance.

Memberships and family accounts

Yearly memberships tracked in-app with booking lockouts for lapsed renewals, and family groups under a single paying account so no set slips through unpaid.

Payments with Square

Cards saved securely with Square, per-set fees and tiered guest day fees charged against each booking, and booking privileges paused when a balance runs more than two weeks overdue.

Boat operations and incidents

Start and end reports with engine hours, fuel level and photo proof, refuel receipts with cost and litres, and automatic incident flags when consecutive bookings' numbers don't reconcile.

05Under the hood

Card data stays with Square, club data stays consistent

Payments run on Square's card-on-file vault, so the club never touches anything beyond the last four digits of a member's card. Charges are calculated server-side from the booking record itself — sets, guests, visit tiers — so what a member sees on their dashboard is what their card is charged, and every payment traces back to a specific booking.

The application runs on Next.js and Supabase, deployed on Vercel, with row-level security separating what members, family payers and committee admins can see and change. Because the boat's engine hours and fuel levels are captured at both ends of every booking, the system can compare consecutive reports and raise an incident automatically when the numbers drift — turning what used to be an argument into a record.
